# Council Transcript — Series 3 Angle Selection
Date: 2026-05-25

---

## Original Question

Which angle is strongest for a 2-3 post longform Substack series on U.S. electricity transmission, given that Series 1 covered gas-to-power price transmission and Series 2 covered the interconnection queue as a broken option-pricing system?

---

## Framed Question

The creator of *Marginal Fuel* is choosing the angle for Series 3 (2-3 posts). Series 1: LMP regression, 238 weeks PJM/Henry Hub. Series 2: interconnection queue as broken option-pricing (speculative entry at <1% of option value; cascade-unstable floating-strike upgrade costs; FERC Order 2023 as partial fix).

Three candidate angles:

**Angle 1: The Congestion Money** — FTRs pay holders when lines stay congested. PJM/MISO auction clearing data public. Firm-level ownership not public. Series would quantify the congestion revenue pool and map persistently congested paths without naming rent-extractors.

**Angle 2: HVDC and the Long Game** — SunZia (3 GW), Grain Belt Express (5 GW), TransWest Express (3 GW). HVDC economics, multi-state siting failures. Infrastructure narrative; political economy tension mostly procedural.

**Angle 3: Order 1920 Mechanics** — FERC May 2024 rule: 20-year regional planning, beneficiary-pays cost allocation. Structural complement to queue reform. Active legal challenges.

Constraints: policy-literate but not electricity-expert audience; public data only; prior series used original quant work; 2-3 posts with 2-3 original figures each.

## Advisor Responses

### The Contrarian

Angle 1 has a structural hole that will embarrass you publicly: you're promising to show "who collects congestion revenue" and then admitting you can't. That's not an analytical gap you can paper over with caveats — it's the entire thesis. Readers who followed your prior series got LMP regressions and barrier option math. Here you're offering a heat map of congested paths and saying "someone profits from this, but we don't know who." That's a downgrade, not a continuation.

Angle 2 is competent infrastructure journalism. It is not quantitative social science. There's no original analytical framework here — it's a project tracker with a physics explainer attached. SunZia got covered by every energy outlet in 2023. Your differentiation is your method, not your topic selection, and HVDC narrative gives you no method.

Angle 3 is where the actual analytical work is, but you're understating the problem: Order 1920's beneficiary-pays allocation is genuinely unsolved. "Long-run avoided cost" attribution across a messy grid is not a back-of-envelope calculation. If you try to operationalize it with public data, you will either produce something too simple to be defensible or something too complex for a 2-3 post series. The legal challenges also mean the rule may not survive — you could publish a rigorous analysis of a vacated order.

The deeper problem: none of these has the clean data-to-insight pipeline that made Series 1 and 2 work. You had Henry Hub prices and LMP outcomes. You had queue withdrawal rates and deposit amounts. What's the equivalent here? Until you can answer that in one sentence, you don't have a series — you have three half-formed pitches.

### The First Principles Thinker

The question you're asking is "which angle is best." The question you should be asking is "what is this series actually about?"

Series 1: price transmission. Series 2: entry incentives and option mispricing. Strip those down and the through-line is: markets fail when the rules create perverse incentives that participants rationally exploit. Gas prices bleed through imperfectly because the market design lets them. The queue is broken because low-cost optionality rewards speculative entry.

So what is transmission, at its core? It's the physical constraint that makes every other dysfunction possible. Congestion isn't an accident — it's a feature for the people collecting FTR revenue. You can't fix the queue if the lines don't get built. You can't build the lines if the cost allocation is contested.

Angles 2 and 3 are consequences. Angle 1 is cause.

The deeper question for Angle 1: who benefits from the constraint? You don't need firm names to answer this. You need to show that congestion rents are persistent, concentrated on predictable paths, and structurally resistant to the new line investment that would eliminate them. That's the argument. FTR data gives you the congestion revenue pool. FERC transmission constraint data gives you the paths. Historical build rates give you the revealed preference: lines aren't getting built where rents are highest.

The series arc then becomes unavoidable: Series 1 showed price signals transmit imperfectly. Series 2 showed new supply can't enter cleanly. Series 3 shows the infrastructure that would fix both is blocked — partly by physics, mostly by incentives. That's a complete theory of why U.S. electricity markets underperform.

Angle 1 isn't the best angle. It's the only angle that completes the argument.

### The Expansionist

Angle 1 is the breakout piece. Here's what everyone is underselling.

The three-series arc you're building is actually a unified theory of why the U.S. grid can't decarbonize at speed — and Angle 1 closes it perfectly. Series 1 showed gas prices flow through. Series 2 showed entry is broken. Angle 1 shows the incumbency rents that make reform politically impossible. That's not three posts about transmission. That's a coherent book proposal.

The hidden upside: FTR congestion revenue is the missing link between transmission economics and regulatory capture — and almost no policy-literate writing has quantified it at the path level. MISO and PJM auction clearing data is genuinely underexploited. If you can map which paths are persistently congested across 3-5 years and back out the aggregate rent pool, you produce a number — call it "the congestion tax on renewables" — that journalists, advocates, and FERC staff will cite. That number is a distribution mechanism. It spreads your readership beyond current subscribers.

The analytical surface is also richer than Angles 2 or 3. You can do time-series of path-level congestion rents, show how they correlate with delayed interconnection queues from Series 2, and build the implied counterfactual: what transmission investment would have eliminated this rent? That ties your original quantitative work across all three series into one figure. A single chart connecting Series 1, 2, and 3 data is the kind of thing that gets screenshot-shared.

Angles 2 and 3 are good journalism. Angle 1 is a franchise.

### The Outsider

Series 1 had a number: 238 weeks of data, a regression, a number that came out. Series 2 had a number: option value, strike price, a percentage. A reader could picture it.

What's the number in Series 3?

Angle 1 has a number. Congestion revenue pool — quantified. Persistently congested paths — mapped. That's something a reader can hold. The "we can show the money but not the face" framing is actually more compelling than naming names. It creates productive tension. The reader understands the system is extracting rent even without a villain.

Angle 2 has no number that belongs to this series. SunZia's 3 GW is someone else's number. The author would be narrating infrastructure, not producing original analysis. That breaks the established pattern.

Angle 3 has no number at all for a general reader. "Beneficiary-pays cost allocation" and "20-year regional planning" are phrases that will cause eyes to glaze over before the second paragraph. The author would be translating regulatory text, not doing original analytical work. The legal challenge angle is news coverage, not original research.

The deeper issue: Series 1 and 2 both had a mechanism the reader hadn't noticed before — price transmission, option mispricing. Angle 1 offers that same mechanism (rent persistence via congestion). Angles 2 and 3 don't.

The outsider answer is Angle 1, and it's not close. The only risk is whether the public data is rich enough to produce figures that feel original rather than descriptive. That's a technical question for someone else on this council. But the conceptual fit is clear.

### The Executor

Angle 1 is the only one with a clear Monday morning task.

Here is the literal first step: go to PJM's Markets & Operations portal, pull the FTR auction results (they publish cleared prices, volumes, and paths going back years), and run a simple pivot — which path-pairs have cleared positive FTR prices most consistently over the last 3-5 years. That identifies the persistent congestion rents in dollar terms. You have your headline figure within a week.

Angle 2 has no first step that produces original analysis. SunZia's route and capacity are already documented. You would be summarizing infrastructure journalism, not doing original quantitative work. The HVDC physics are real but the data is in press releases and FERC filings, not structured datasets. You cannot build a figure that shows something nobody has shown before.

Angle 3 is pure regulatory text analysis. FERC Order 1920 is 1,300+ pages. The "original work" would be reading it carefully and synthesizing — that is a law review article, not a quant Substack post. The legal challenges are active, meaning the story is unresolved and will date badly.

Back to Angle 1: MISO also publishes FTR auction data. Running the same congestion persistence analysis across both ISOs gives you cross-market comparison and a second figure. The limitation — firm-level ownership is private — is actually a feature, not a bug. You quantify the rent pool, show which paths are persistently congested, and the absence of ownership data becomes the policy point: public money, private beneficiaries, no disclosure requirement. That is the story.

Angle 1 is executable. The others are not, at least not to the analytical standard the prior series set.

## Chairman's Synthesis

### Where the Council Agrees

Angle 1 is the unanimous first choice. It has a number (congestion revenue, quantified at the path level), a mechanism readers haven't seen before (rent persistence via deliberate under-investment), and it completes the three-series arc that Angles 2 and 3 cannot. The Executor won the peer reviews for one reason: it proved executability rather than asserting it — the PJM FTR portal exists, the data goes back years, and a path-pair pivot produces a headline figure in a week.

The council also agreed on why Angles 2 and 3 fail. Angle 2 produces infrastructure journalism, not original analysis; SunZia's 3 GW is someone else's number. Angle 3 is a 1,300-page regulatory text sitting on top of a live legal challenge — the story may be vacated before it publishes.

### Where the Council Clashes

The one real disagreement: is the ownership data gap fatal (the Contrarian) or a feature (everyone else)? The Contrarian argued you cannot credibly promise to show who extracts congestion revenue and then admit you cannot name them. Everyone else argued you never needed to name them — the prior series never named specific market participants either. The Contrarian is applying a standard the series never set.

### Blind Spots the Council Caught

**Pedagogy (caught by all 5 reviewers).** The council debated analytical method and series coherence and ignored whether a policy-literate non-expert can follow FTR mechanics. Series 1 mapped one concept (gas prices → LMPs). Series 2 mapped one concept (options → deposits). Angle 1 requires simultaneously understanding a financial instrument (FTR) and the physical constraint it references (transmission congestion). That is a heavier pedagogical lift than either prior series — probably 400-600 words of setup before any number appears.

**Post count feasibility (caught by Reviewer 4).** A single congestion-rent pivot is one post, not a series. The council endorsed Angle 1 without testing whether it generates enough structural depth for 6-9 original figures across 2-3 posts.

### The Recommendation

Angle 1. Not close. But it requires three distinct analytical threads:

**Post 1 — The congestion revenue pool.** Quantify path-level FTR rents across PJM and MISO over 3-5 years. Which paths clear positive prices year after year? What is the aggregate rent pool? This is the number. This is the headline.

**Post 2 — The investment gap.** Take the congested paths from Post 1 and show where new transmission has and has not been built. Revealed preference: lines are not being built where congestion rents are highest. FERC constraint data and historical build records are public. This post connects rents to the physical under-investment that sustains them.

**Post 3 — The closing arc figure.** The one chart that connects all three series. Series 1: price signals transmit imperfectly. Series 2: new supply cannot enter cleanly. Series 3: the infrastructure that would fix both is blocked by the same incumbent incentive structure.

On pedagogy: Post 1 needs an extended setup section — probably 400-600 words — before any number appears.

On the Contrarian's objection: do not drop Angle 1. Be explicit in the framing — public auctions, private ownership, no disclosure. That is not a gap in the analysis. It is the finding.

### The One Thing to Do First

Go to the PJM Markets & Operations portal, pull FTR auction clearing results for the last five years, and build a pivot table: path-pair by year, average cleared FTR price, total MWh. Sort descending by aggregate implied revenue. The paths at the top — clearing positive prices year after year — are the persistent congestion rents. That table is Post 1's backbone and the factual test of whether the series is viable. Run that pivot before writing a single word.
